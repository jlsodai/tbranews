Global Partnerships

Bringing Our Business-Led Model to the Future of Humanitarian Investing

Drawing on our work with displaced communities across Africa, we recently contributed to the World Economic Forum's Humanitarian and Resilience Investing (HRI) Initiative, sharing insights on how market-based approaches can unlock long term economic opportunity. The HRI Roadmap for Africa, a collaborative strategy to unlock sustainable private sector investment in Africa's frontier markets, reinforces many of the principles that guide our work, highlighting the need for governments, businesses, financial institutions, development partners and local communities to work together to transform fragmented interventions into coordinated systems that drive sustainable growth.

 

“

The future of cooperation lies in building markets, not managing dependency. Instead of asking, ‘How do we help refugees?’ we ask, ‘How do we grow economies that include them?’

 

Isaac Kwaku Fokuo Jr. — Founder & Curator, Amahoro Coalition

As global conversations continue to shift from aid to investment, we remain committed to demonstrating how business-led partnerships can unlock opportunities for displaced people while contributing to Africa's broader economic development.

Government Partnership

A Strategic Partnership to Expand Economic Inclusion in Nigeria

Amahoro Coalition has signed a three-year strategic partnership with Nigeria's National Commission for Refugees, Migrants and Internally Displaced Persons (NCFRMI), reinforcing a shared commitment to place the private sector at the center of economic inclusion. Signed in Abuja, the agreement will strengthen collaboration between government and business to expand employment, entrepreneurship and investment opportunities for refugees, internally displaced persons, migrants and returnees. Through the partnership, Amahoro Coalition will convene business leaders, support policy dialogue and help unlock market-driven solutions that create sustainable livelihoods.

 

“

Across Africa, we have seen that when refugees and other displaced populations are given the opportunity to work, start businesses and contribute to local economies, entire communities benefit. Through this partnership with NCFRMI, we will mobilize business leaders, attract investment, and create pathways that enable displaced people in Nigeria to thrive as workers, entrepreneurs and contributors to economic growth.

 

Bathsheba Asati — Principal Strategy Custodian, Amahoro Coalition

Private Sector Engagement

Exploring Inclusive Markets with Uganda's Private Sector

We recently brought together some of Uganda's leading private sector leaders for a conversation on the economic opportunities within Uganda, Africa's largest refugee-hosting country. Drawing on insights from our latest research, the discussion challenged traditional narratives around displacement, highlighting that behind the humanitarian statistics are viable customers, skilled workers and entrepreneurs already contributing to local economies. Together, participants explored the structural barriers that hold these markets back and the commercial opportunities that emerge when businesses choose to engage.

 

“

The opportunity already exists. Our role is to help businesses see it, understand it and build the partnerships needed to unlock it. When the private sector recognizes displaced people as participants in the economy rather than beneficiaries of aid, everyone stands to gain.

 

Tito Mbathi — Strategy Custodian for Partnerships, Amahoro Coalition

The conversation reflects our continued engagement in Uganda, where we are working with companies that are creating employment opportunities for refugees and host communities while supporting a growing pipeline of refugee entrepreneurs, including the largest number in the newest cohort of the Amahoro Fellowship.

Fellowship Onboarding

Cohort 4 Comes Together in Naivasha for Fellowship Onboarding

The newest cohort of the Amahoro Fellowship is now coming together in Naivasha, Kenya, for an expanded six-day onboarding intensive designed to lay the foundation for the journey ahead. More than an orientation, the programme immerses Fellows in the values, leadership principles and community that define the Fellowship. Through interactive workshops, peer learning, storytelling and hands-on challenges, the experience helps Fellows build meaningful relationships, clarify their leadership purpose and prepare for the opportunities and responsibilities that lie ahead.

Structured around six themes: Land, Belong, Lead, Root, Build, Brand and Move, the intensive gives Fellows the practical skills and support they need to grow their initiatives while building strong connections with one another. Sessions cover African leadership and Ubuntu, entrepreneurship, operational excellence, communications, fundraising, stakeholder engagement and resilience, complemented by industry immersions, cultural exchanges and conversations with alumni and mentors. By the time the programme concludes, Fellows leave not only with stronger ventures and clearer plans for growth, but also as part of a lifelong pan-African network of leaders committed to creating economic opportunities for displaced people and their host communities.

Regional Intensive

West Africa Fellows Gather in Abuja

Fourteen Amahoro Fellows from Nigeria, Ghana, Togo and Cameroon gathered in Abuja for the West Africa Regional Intensive, bringing together entrepreneurs building businesses in agriculture, mining, healthcare, renewable energy, manufacturing, recycling, digital commerce and financial services.

For five days, Fellows compared notes on what it takes to build in the region. Mary Katambi spoke about the logistics challenge facing her growing e-commerce platform. Nzem Luka shared how artificial intelligence is beginning to change mineral processing. Noel Balogun Samuel reflected on why he now believes social impact and profitability can go hand in hand.

They also heard from alumni who have already walked parts of that road. Agwu Kalu Ibe, President of the Amahoro United Alumni Network and founder of LevelUp Recyclers, spoke about growing a recycling business from an idea banks once dismissed into an enterprise employing 670 people. “I don’t want the next cohort to make the same mistakes we made,” he told the room.

The Fellowship now brings together 129 entrepreneurs whose ventures support more than 2,244 jobs across Africa.
