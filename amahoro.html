#AccelerateAction: Advancing Gender Equality for Displaced Women across Africa

As we mark International Women's Day 2025, the urgency for gender equality has never been clearer. At the current pace, it will take until 2158 (five generations from now) to achieve full gender parity, according to the World Economic Forum. This staggering timeline is a stark reminder that we cannot afford to wait. The fight for equality demands bold, decisive action to dismantle the systemic barriers and biases that continue to hold women back, particularly in underserved and displaced communities, where the challenges are even more pronounced.

In this edition, we spotlight how Amahoro Coalition, along with our clients and partners, is driving real change, creating employment opportunities, championing women’s leadership, and paving the way for a more just and equitable future.

Join us at the upcoming Africa Forum on Displacement taking place in Nairobi from 28 to 29 April 2025 to accelerate the momentum for gender equality, in the workplace and in displaced communities, because five generations is far too long to wait.

Invisible Architects of Change: How African women are slowly redefining Philanthropy


African women have long been the invisible architects of societal progress, driving philanthropy through informal, trust-based networks. A case in point is Ethiopia’s Debo (mutual aid groups) where women contributed labour and formed part of the group’s leadership to uplift farming communities. Today, within formalized, large-scale giving structures, African women continue to embed philanthropy in their daily lives through women-led organizations such as the African Women’s Development Fund (AWDF) and the Sirleaf Market Women’s Fund (SMWF). For example, AWDF has disbursed $100 million in grants to more than 1,500 organizations to address systemic issues such as limited access to education and poverty, on a global scale.

Our latest research reveals that as Africa navigates a changing aid landscape, the mobilization of local resources and expertise has become essential. African philanthropists are now uniquely positioned to redefine how development is conceived, funded, and implemented. Examining this closely, particularly through the lens of African women's philanthropy, it becomes evident that despite its potential, systemic barriers such as bureaucratic business registration processes and rigid legal frameworks continue to hinder women’s philanthropic capacity.

Nourishing Futures: One Woman's Mission to Transform Displaced Communities


The first 1,000 days of a child's life are critical. For mothers in displaced communities, these days are often marked by uncertainty, limited resources, and constant struggle. Esther knew this intimately. Having witnessed the nutritional challenges facing mothers in refugee settlements, she refused to accept the status quo.

Her solution? Wise Baby Porridge—a locally-produced, nutrient-rich food designed to combat malnutrition and empower women entrepreneurs. But this was more than just a product. It was a revolution.

"Women produce 70% of Africa's food," Esther explains. "Yet we're systematically denied the resources to truly transform our communities. Wise Baby Porridge is about changing that narrative."

Her innovation does more than feed children. It creates economic opportunities for women in displaced communities. Each package represents employment, skills training, and a pathway to economic independence. Women who were once marginalized are now becoming small-scale entrepreneurs, distribution agents, and community leaders.

With Africa's population projected to reach 2.5 billion by 2050, Esther understands that investing in women is investing in the continent's future. Her work through the Amahoro Fellowship demonstrates how targeted, compassionate innovation can accelerate action and drive systemic change.

Bridging the Financial Gap: Why Collaboration is Key to Africa’s Financial Future


With 60% of women in Africa still unbanked, financial exclusion remains a major economic barrier, particularly for displaced women and their host communities. When women lack access to financial services, entire ecosystems of economic resilience weaken. To address this, Africa’s financial industry must move beyond competition and embrace collaboration. Rather than operating in silos, fintechs, banks, microfinance institutions, and telecoms must work together to unlock inclusive financial growth.

Bathsheba Asati, Principal Strategy Custodian at Amahoro Coalition, and Kwadwo Adjei-Barwuah, Head of Investment at Growth Investment Partners, share key insights from a recent roundtable discussion hosted alongside the Africa Financial Summit (AFIS) in Casablanca, Morocco, on how partnerships can bridge these financial gaps.
