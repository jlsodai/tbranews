Overview of the Forum

The Africa Forum on Displacement 2025 was more than a convening; it was a statement of intent. Held across Kakuma and Nairobi, it brought together more than 400 delegates in person and over 1,100 virtual participants from 30 countries, uniting governments, the private sector, humanitarian agencies, and refugee-led organizations.

#ALLIn

The #ALLIn theme that guided the Forum reaffirmed a fundamental truth:

Displaced people are not a humanitarian burden, they are an untapped source of innovation, resilience, and economic potential.



What are they up to now

When we met Kiza in Kakuma on the margins of AFD2025, he was proudly showcasing the work that began in 2022 with just a laptop and a bold vision. Today, his initiative, Action for Refugee Learning (AReL), has grown into a dynamic digital skills hub after receiving support from Amahoro Coalition and WUSC, enabling access to laptops, solar power, and reliable internet.

AReL has now trained hundreds of refugees in digital marketing, e-commerce, data analysis, cybersecurity and full-stack development, with graduates already securing paid projects and jobs across several countries.

Quarterly Insights

As we move forward, this update marks the beginning of a quarterly series designed to keep the conversation active and focused.

Each edition will look back at the commitments made in April, track progress across the displacement and inclusion landscape in Africa, and highlight the emerging issues that should shape dialogue at the next Africa Forum on Displacement in Q3 of 2026.


What was the most consequential conversation at AFD 2025?


Watch highlight

For some, it was the moment a refugee entrepreneur in Kakuma connected with investors in the Marketplace of Solutions, where ventures in renewable energy, fashion, agriculture, and digital innovation proved the depth of talent across displaced communities. For others, it was hearing how financing models from IFC and Equity Bank can make financial inclusion a viable growth strategy for banks across Africa. Policy leaders found clarity in roundtables on how national frameworks like Kenya’s Shirika Plan can embed refugee inclusion in long-term development. Private-sector managers discovered new ways to build inclusive supply chains and create jobs at scale, while refugee-led organizations reaffirmed that displaced people are not beneficiaries but partners in progress.

Across every venue, one message tied these conversations together: AFD 2025 marked a shift from aid to agency. As we move toward AFD 2026, we look forward to hearing what actions, partnerships, and solutions you are driving forward.




Where do we go from here?


The journey continues, and planning for the next Africa Forum on Displacement is already underway. The 2026 edition, with dates and venue to be announced soon, will build on the momentum of AFD 2025, deepen this year’s themes, and spotlight the progress taking shape across the continent. It will also push the conversation forward by exploring how finance, technology, and continental frameworks such as the AfCFTA can accelerate inclusion at scale.


This next phase is especially critical because the work advanced at AFD will be reviewed alongside global commitments. As stakeholders of the Global Compact on Refugees (GCR), the co-conveners will participate in the Global Refugee Forum Progress Review from 15 to 17 December 2025, a key milestone that assesses how countries and partners are fulfilling pledges under the GCR. Aligning AFD conversations with this global process ensures that Africa’s priorities remain visible, coordinated, and influential as progress is evaluated and future commitments are shaped.
