2025 Has Been a Year of Scaling What Works


2025 was a year of scale, execution and tangible impact for Amahoro Coalition. We expanded our footprint across Africa, deepened private sector engagement, and delivered measurable results that demonstrate the economic case for inclusion. Through partnerships with over 380 companies, we secured more than 240,000 job commitments, while advancing large-scale initiatives such as our collaboration with the Bank of Agriculture in Nigeria targeting 200,000 jobs.

A defining milestone was the third Africa Forum on Displacement, which brought together over 1,500 participants across in-person and virtual formats, with private sector actors making up more than half of attendees. The Forum marked a clear shift from aid dependency to market-driven solutions, unlocking $308 million in commitments and over 32,000 pledged jobs.

We also launched the Skills Hub, a first-of-its-kind platform connecting displaced talent to employers across Africa, already onboarding over 6,000 talents and supporting more than 2,000 into work. Alongside this, the Pathways to Employment series across 15 countries is setting a new standard for how refugee employment is understood and enabled.

Across key markets such as Nigeria and Somalia, these efforts are translating into real systems change, proving that economic inclusion is not only possible, but scalable.

How Amahoro Fellows Are Scaling Businesses and Creating Jobs Across Africa


Tilda Mwai (M&E and Analytics) and Julius Muriuki (Client Sourcing and Engagement) with Amahoro Fellow Espoir Bahirwe (centre) at his plantation in Lubumbashi.


Across Kenya, Uganda, Ethiopia, the Democratic Republic of Congo, Ghana, Nigeria, and South Africa, our recent field visits showed that refugee-led businesses are not waiting for opportunity. They are already building it. What stands out on the ground is not potential alone, but businesses with customers, revenues, and clear paths to scale if the right capital and partnerships are in place.

In Lubumbashi, DRC, Espoir Bahirwe's Agrownest is already employing 136 internally displaced people across its agricultural operations and is now moving into maize processing to capture more value locally and create new roles in milling, packaging, warehousing, and distribution.

In Accra, Grace Bogui’s Attiéké Love has grown into a thriving food business generating about GHS 58,000 (approx. $2,300) a month, supporting five staff directly and creating more than 250 income opportunities across its value chain, with plans to expand production and link over 30 cassava farmers into local supply.

In Cape Town, Ariane’s Oasis Inclusive Center now generates close to $10,000 a month through autism-focused school transport and inclusive tourism, employing four full-time staff and up to 15 part-time drivers. With a second vehicle, the business could double transport capacity and create at least six more jobs.

Labour Mobility Is the Missing Link in Africa’s Displacement Response



As global development funding declines, displacement is increasingly being shaped by policy choices rather than resources alone. Our latest position paper examines this shift and makes one point clear: current systems are not aligned with the realities of labour mobility across Africa.

Across the region, countries are hosting growing numbers of displaced people while facing shrinking external support. In 2023, humanitarian funding covered only 44% of needs, exposing a widening gap between commitments and reality. At the same time, most displacement happens within Africa itself, with countries in the region hosting up to 70% of those on the move.

What we see in practice is a policy contradiction. While regional frameworks encourage labour mobility, national systems often restrict access to work and movement for displaced populations. These restrictions are not accidental. They are driven by real economic concerns, particularly in labour markets where low skilled workers compete for the same opportunities. The result is a system that limits participation instead of enabling it.

The opportunity ahead is clear. Aligning labour mobility systems with economic realities can unlock access to jobs, reduce dependency, and create more efficient labour markets across the region.
